Finally?!

Although I have now been in Peru for more than two months, I haven’t managed to publish a single article so far – until now. Today, I’d like to look back on the few days we spent in Lima, which served as preparation for my placement in Miguel Grau.

Everything began on Friday, August 22. The departure for the twenty other volunteers and me was scheduled for 2:45 p.m., flying from Frankfurt to Lima via Bogotá. Exhausted, occasionally travel-sick, and burdened with my three-piece luggage, we arrived at around three in the morning at our hostel in Lima, the Flying Dog.

The first thing that made us laugh was the room layout, which still felt completely absurd to us at that point. One of the windows, for example, looked straight onto an aloe vera plant hanging against a brick wall. We quickly got in touch with our neighbors—fortunately also volunteers—since the wall between the rooms didn’t reach the ceiling and even had an open window built into it.

After only a few hours of sleep, our schedule resumed on Sunday morning. We met Doris, our national coordinator from Ecoselva, who would be accompanying us throughout the year. We spent several hours in a meeting room, where she shared a wide range of information about the year ahead and our stay in Lima. Although we had already completed a ten-day preparation seminar before departure, this meeting proved extremely helpful—especially because Doris shared her many years of experience living in Peru.

In the afternoon, we explored Miraflores, one of Lima’s more affluent districts. It is heavily oriented toward tourism and also the neighborhood where our hostel was located. This was where I saw the Pacific Ocean for the first time—and, unfortunately, where I soaked my first and nicest of the four pairs of trousers I had brought with me in salty seawater.

We also explored the area around the hostel: a small bar serving excellent pisco sours, several large green parks with free-roaming cats that you could pet (and, at least in theory, even adopt), and the multi-day coffee market known as Semana del Café.

During these preparation days, our meals were organized by Ecoselva. We had lunch at an Arab restaurant and dinner at a Chinese one. The latter, unfortunately, didn’t convince most of us. Looking back, I’m sure we would handle it much better today. It was less a matter of taste than of adjustment—our bodies and expectations simply hadn’t arrived in Peru yet.

Adding to this was the fact that I had suggested ordering an aloe vera drink, so that we could try everything that was on offer. That drink didn’t quite match our taste at the time, and the restaurant ended up with a rather mixed reputation within our group—probably also because everything felt so unexpected.

Sunday, August 24, 2025

Far from the watchful eyes of security guards and the sweeping brooms of cleaning crews, we discovered a different side of Lima today: a city of around eleven million inhabitants, marked by stark social contrasts.

We traveled to the district of Villa María del Triunfo, a so-called pueblo joven—a structurally disadvantaged neighborhood, primarily inhabited by migrants from the Andes and often built through grassroots initiative. The journey itself was rough: in Peru, traffic laws are more of a suggestion, seatbelts are often decorative, and the horn is used far more frequently than the brake. For us newcomers from Germany, it initially felt chaotic, but it is simply part of everyday life here.

On site, we were introduced to a project focused on urban agriculture. Around fifteen years ago, a group of women decided to make their surroundings more livable. Since construction is prohibited beneath high-voltage power lines, they obtained permission to grow fruits and vegetables there instead. The beginnings were difficult, as few people believed in the project’s success. To get started at all, they built a makeshift fence out of tires, wooden planks, and corrugated metal to protect the gardens from theft.

Today, the project has expanded to several neighborhoods and includes many families cultivating small plots of land. Some sell their produce, while others grow food solely for their own consumption. What they all share is the care and dedication invested in their gardens. At lunchtime, we were invited to another project garden to enjoy a freshly prepared meal made from homegrown vegetables—an absolute highlight.

Afterward, we returned to the hostel, moving from the quiet, communal gardens back into the busy, noisy streets of Miraflores. There, I was able to fully enjoy the final day of Semana del Café. Hidden between the warm aroma of coffee and the bustling crowd, I met several interesting people, including a tattoo artist who was creating temporary coffee-themed tattoos for visitors. Theo and I were welcome conversation partners, and we spent quite some time talking with him about his life and work in Peru—giving him a well-earned break from the market’s constant activity.

Later that evening, I also exchanged contact details with a few stallholders, from whom I bought sweaters, a waist bag, and a new pair of trousers. One thing became very clear during these days: when packing for the year ahead, I hadn’t always made the best decisions.

I had carefully kept a packing list and eventually ended up with one that included everything I truly needed. But shortly before departure, I suddenly began negotiating that list with myself—using surprisingly poor arguments. Sweaters? I don’t need that many. Games? Who would I play with in a small village? Warm hiking clothes? I won’t be doing that in Peru. Well… I was very wrong and had to come up with creative stopgap solutions.

Monday, August 25, 2025

We were supposed to meet a staff member from the German embassy today, who would provide us with further information about the country and our volunteer IDs. In Peru, many everyday processes require an identification number known as the DNI. It is used for Yape—a digital payment method comparable to PayPal—as well as in hostels or for larger purchases.

The meeting was canceled at short notice because the staff member fell ill. A blessing in disguise: with the extra time, we were able to buy SIM cards and finally reconnect with the outside world. We were now more independent and could move around the city more freely.

We took the bus to Lima’s historic center. Anyone who complains about overcrowded buses or subways in Germany wouldn’t even manage to get on a bus in Lima. They were so packed that you had to twist and squeeze more than in a game of Twister. Getting off the bus was an experience in itself, usually consisting of little more than the words “¡Bajo!” and “Pásame.”

The historic center itself impressed me deeply. Most of the buildings date back to the period of Spanish colonial rule, which began in 1535. Lima was founded as the “City of Kings” and long served as the seat of the Viceroy of Peru—one of the most important political and economic centers of the Spanish Empire in South America. The Church, the administration, and wealthy colonial elites invested in grand churches, imposing palaces, and richly decorated homes, using local materials such as brick, wood, and stone. All of this is clearly reflected in this UNESCO World Heritage Site.

We ended the evening at Parque de la Reserva, where we watched an impressive fountain show while enjoying churros and fairground attractions.

Tuesday, August 26, 2025

After another meeting with Doris, we visited the tourist district of Barranco today. We followed the coastal promenade and enjoyed the view of the Pacific, where I went swimming along with only a few others. Because of the cold, the swimmers later returned to the hostel by taxi. In the evening, we had empanadas—pastry pockets filled with cheese, chicken, or vegetables. For me, they were a good substitute for the kebab I wouldn’t be enjoying for the next year.

For the final day, nothing else was planned, as everyone was leaving Lima at different times and Doris wanted to be present for all departures. In the afternoon, Erik and I met David, the head of CACI Satinaki—the place where we would live and work. He explained many things to us, from the people there to travel opportunities during the year.

That same evening, we set off. Long-distance travel in Peru is largely done by bus, and the level of comfort is noticeably higher than in Germany. There are large terminals with luggage check-in, and the buses themselves are surprisingly comfortable: wide seats, plenty of legroom, and backrests that recline up to 160 or even 180 degrees. The eleven-hour journey to Pichanaki was therefore quite enjoyable.

What happened there will follow in the next post.

Looking back, those first days were overwhelming—but wonderful. Whether it was city life, the food, or the stark contrasts between different neighborhoods, I slowly realized that I had arrived in a culture unfamiliar to me, one in which I would spend an entire year. A year I’m genuinely looking forward to—for precisely that reason: cultural exchange.

Stay tuned, Buena semana y hasta la próxima!
